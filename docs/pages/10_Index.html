Theoretical Background

Selecting individuals, both for product development and parental selection, based on multiple traits is a rather common practice in plant breeding. That selection is typically done based on an index that summarises all available information for a genotype into a single value.

A selection index for \(t\) standardized traits is defined as

\[ \mathbf{I} = \sum_{k = 1}^tb_ky_k = \mathbf{b}'\mathbf{y} \]

where \(b\) represents the index coeffficients. Typically, it is assumed that \(\sigma_I^2 = 1\), thus

\[ \operatorname{var}(\mathbf{b'y}) = \mathbf{b'}\operatorname{var}(\mathbf{y})\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{b'Pb} \]

Under the multitrait framework, \(\operatorname{var}(\mathbf{y})\) refers to the phenotypic variance covariance matrix between traits so that \(P_{jk} = \operatorname{cov}(y_j, y_k)\). The response to truncation selection on the index follows the breeders equation so that

\[ R_{g_j} = \frac{i}{\sigma_I}\operatorname{cov}(g_j, I) = i\sum_{k = 1}^t\operatorname{cov}(g_j, y_k)b_k = i\sum_{k = 1}^t\Gamma_{jk} b_k \]

It is important to notice that under BLUP selection, the formulation of the index changes to

\[ \mathbf{I} = \sum_{k = 1}^tb_k\hat{g}_k = \mathbf{b}'\mathbf{\hat{g}} \]

Therefore, the variance of the index now represents \(\sigma_I^2 = 1 = \mathbf{b'}\operatorname{var}(\mathbf{\hat{g}})\mathbf{b}\) so that \(P_{jk} = \operatorname{cov}(\hat{g}_j, \hat{g}_k)\). Additionally, the response would be defined as

\[ R_{g_j} = i\sum_{k = 1}^t\operatorname{cov}(g_j, \hat{g}_k)b_k = i\sum_{k = 1}^t\Gamma_{jk} b_k \]

In this particular case, given the properties of the BLUP \(\operatorname{var}(\hat{\mathbf{g}}) = \operatorname{cov}(\mathbf{g}, \hat{\mathbf{g}})\), \(\Gamma_{jk} = \operatorname{cov}(g_j, \hat{g}_k) = P_{jk}\)

An standardize response for each trait can be obtained by mutiplying by the inverse of a diagonal matrix \(H\) of the genetic standard deviations

\[ x = i\mathbf{H}^{-1}\mathbf{\Gamma b} \]

From where we can solve for the index to obtain that

\[ \mathbf{b} = i^{-1}\mathbf{\Gamma}^{-1} \mathbf{H x} \]

Desired Gain Index

The desired gains coefficients represent the vector of standardize response that we would like to achive for each of the traits of interest. However, normally these gains cannot be achieved, since the resopnse must satisfy that \(\mathbf{b'Pb} = 1\) and therefore the following equation

\[ \begin{align} i^{-1} \, \mathbf{x}^\top \mathbf{H} \mathbf{\Gamma}^{-1} \mathbf{P} \mathbf{\Gamma}^{-1} \mathbf{H} \mathbf{x} \, i^{-1} &= 1 \\ i^{-2} \, \mathbf{x}^\top \mathbf{H} \mathbf{\Gamma}^{-1} \mathbf{P} \mathbf{\Gamma}^{-1} \mathbf{H} \mathbf{x} &= 1 \\ \mathbf{x}^\top \mathbf{H} \mathbf{\Gamma}^{-1} \mathbf{P} \mathbf{\Gamma}^{-1} \mathbf{H} \mathbf{x} &= i^2 \\ \mathbf{x}^\top \mathbf{Q} \mathbf{x} &= i^2 \end{align} \]

The equation above \(\mathbf{Q} = \mathbf{H} \mathbf{\Gamma}^{-1} \mathbf{P} \mathbf{\Gamma}^{-1} \mathbf{H}\) represents the matrix representation of an ellipsoide (high dimensional ellipse). The shape and surface of the ellipse (if only 2 traits are considered) is determined by the intensity of selection, the correlation between traits the heritability and the accuracy of each one of them.

The response thus represents the intersection between a vector director (the desired vector) and an ellipse (or ellipsoide). This, mathematically, is obtained as follows

First, the response is defined as the desired vector times a proportionality constant \(t\)

\[ \mathbf{x} = t\mathbf{d} \]

Then, we substitute in the constraint equation and obtain

\[ (t\mathbf{d})'\mathbf{Q}t\mathbf{d} = i^2 \\ t^2\mathbf{d}'\mathbf{Qd} = i^2 \\ t = \frac{i}{\sqrt{\mathbf{d}'\mathbf{Qd}}} \]

And therefore, the maximized response is given by \(\mathbf{x} = \frac{i}{\sqrt{\mathbf{d}'\mathbf{Qd}}} \mathbf{d}\), which represents where does the vector of desired gains touch the surface of the ellipse.

Genetic Merit

A different approach is to specify the relative importance of genetic gains for each trait, which is known as the economic weights or merit coefficients. The merit function is defined as follows.

\[ M = \sum_{k = 1}^t a_k(\frac{g_k}{\sigma_{gk}}) = \mathbf{a'H^{-1}g} \]

Now, the merit coefficients measure not the genetic gains but its value. The goal is then to maximize the correlation between the merit or genotypic worth and the Index, agiain, subkject to \(\sigma^2_I = 1\). Therefore, the quantity to be maximized is

\[ \begin{align} \operatorname{cov}(\mathbf{M, I}) &= \operatorname{cov}(\mathbf{a'H^{-1}g}, \mathbf{b'y}) \\ &= \mathbf{a'H^{-1}}\operatorname{cov}(\mathbf{g}, \mathbf{g})\mathbf{b}' \\ &= \mathbf{a'H^{-1}} \mathbf{\Gamma b'} \end{align} \]

Thus, the optimization problem can be defined as

\[ \begin{aligned} \text{maximize} \quad & \mathbf{a'H^{-1} \Gamma b'} \\ \text{subject to} \quad & \mathbf{b' P b = 1} \end{aligned} \]

We can obtain the Lagrange and then solve for b by taking its partial derivative and equating to 0 so that

\[ \begin{aligned} \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{b}, \lambda) &= \mathbf{a'H^{-1} \Gamma b'} - \frac{\lambda}{2} (\mathbf{b' P b} - 1) \\[2mm] \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{b}} &= \mathbf{\Gamma' H^{-1} a} - \lambda \mathbf{P b} = \mathbf{0} \\[1mm] \mathbf{P b} &= \frac{1}{\lambda} \mathbf{\Gamma' H^{-1} a} \\[1mm] \mathbf{b} &= \frac{1}{\lambda} \mathbf{P^{-1} \Gamma' H^{-1} a} \end{aligned} \]

If we substitute this \(b\) values into the Index equation, we would obtain that

\[ I_{SH} = \mathbf{a'H^{-1}\Gamma P^{-1}y} = \frac{\mathbf{a'H^{-1}\hat{\mathbf{g}}}}{\lambda} \]

Additionally, if we substitute in the constraint

\[ \begin{align*} \mathbf{b' P b} &= 1 \\[1mm] \mathbf{\frac{1}{\lambda} a' H^{-1} \Gamma P^{-1} P P^{-1} \Gamma H^{-1} a \frac{1}{\lambda}} &= 1 \\[1mm] \frac{\mathbf{a' H^{-1} \Gamma P^{-1} \Gamma H^{-1} a}}{\lambda^2} &= 1 \\[1mm] \frac{1}{\lambda} &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mathbf{a' Q^{-1} a}}} \end{align*} \]

We can now subsitute that into the coefficients

\[ \mathbf{b} = \frac{1}{\lambda} \mathbf{P^{-1} \Gamma' H^{-1} a} = \frac{\mathbf{P^{-1} \Gamma' H^{-1} a}}{\sqrt{\mathbf{a' Q^{-1} a}}} \]

To obtain \(\mathbf{P}\) and \(\mathbf{\Gamma}\) we need to fit the following multitrait model

\[ \mathbf{y} = \mathbf{\mu} + \mathbf{X \beta} + \mathbf{Z g} + \mathbf{\varepsilon} \]

assuming that \(\mathbf{g} \sim MVN(0, \mathbf{G})\), \(\mathbf{\varepsilon} \sim MVN(0, \mathbf{R})\) and \(\mathbf{y} \sim MVN(\mathbf{X \beta}, \mathbf{V})\), where \(\mathbf{G} = \mathbf{I}_n \otimes \mathbf{\Sigma_g}\) and \(\mathbf{R} = \mathbf{I}_{nt} \otimes \mathbf{\Sigma_e}\).

We will use asreml and sommer to estimates the variance-covariance matrices \(\mathbf{\Sigma_g}\) and \(\mathbf{\Sigma_e}\). For the genetic correlation we will assume and unstructure variance covariance matrix, meaning that the model will estimate \(n*(n+2)/2\) paremeters. In this case, it will estimate a variance for each of the traits plus a covaraince components between them. For the residuals, sommer (at least mmes) does not allow to estimate covariances between the errors for the different traits. Thus, a diagonal matrix will be used. For asreml, an additional unstructured matrix will be employed.

highlight the importance of estimating the correlation between error amongst traits and how does the choice of that variance covariance matrix determine the estimation of the additive correlation between traits.

As mentioned above,\(\mathbf{\Gamma}\) represents the genotypic variance covariance matrix between traits (meaning, its a \(t \times t\) matrix). If only random effects are considered, it is computed as described by Legarra 2016 and Endelman 2023

\[ E[V_y] = \overline{\operatorname{diag}(\mathbf{L})} - \overline{L_{..}} \]

where \(\mathbf{L}\) represents the marginal variance of BLUPs with genotypes nested traits. However, right now the information in the marginal variance matrix is organized with traits nested genotypes, meaning that the diagonal is composed of \(n\) \(t \times t\) variance covariance matrices each genotype for the two traits

\[ \operatorname{var}(\hat{\mathbf{g}}) = \begin{bmatrix} M_{g_1} & \cdots & M_{g_1g_n} \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ M_{g_ng_1} & \cdots & M_{g_n} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \underbrace{ \begin{bmatrix} \sigma^2_{t_1} & \sigma_{t_1 t_2} \\ \sigma_{t_2 t_1} & \sigma^2_{t_2} \end{bmatrix} }_{g_1} & \cdots & M_{g_1g_n} \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ M_{g_ng_1} & \cdots & \underbrace{ \begin{bmatrix} \sigma^2_{t_1} & \sigma_{t_1 t_2} \\ \sigma_{t_2 t_1} & \sigma^2_{t_2} \end{bmatrix} }_{g_n} \end{bmatrix} \]

The reason why this happens is because of how we computed \(\mathbf{G}\) taking into account the kronecker product properties

\[ \mathbf{G} = \mathbf{I_n} \otimes \mathbf{\Sigma_g} = \mathbf{I_n} \otimes \begin{bmatrix} \Sigma_{g,11} & \Sigma_{g,12} \\ \Sigma_{g,21} & \Sigma_{g,22} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{\Sigma_g} & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & \mathbf{\Sigma_g} & \cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & \mathbf{\Sigma_g} \end{bmatrix} \]

The same structure is thus found for \(\operatorname{var}(\mathbf{\hat{g}})\) In summary

idx <- split(1:nt, f = rep(1:t, times = n))

As stated in StageWise documentation, the quadratic matrix can be computed as \(\mathbf{Q} = \mathbf{U}'\mathbf{U}\), where \(\mathbf{U} = \mathbf{R} \mathbf{\Gamma}^{-1} \mathbf{H}\) and \(\mathbf{R}\) is the upper triangular cholesky factor of \(\mathbf{P}\). Therefore

U <- chol(P) %*% solve(Gamma) %*% H

Desired

intensity <- 1

merit <- c(1, 0)
x <- Variable(t)
constraints <- list(quad_form(x, Q) <= intensity^2)
v <- matrix(merit, nrow = 1)
objective <- Maximize(v %*% x)
problem <- Problem(objective, constraints)
result <- solve(problem)
x <- as.numeric(result$getValue(x))
b <- (solve(Gamma) %*% H %*% x) / intensity
b <- b / sqrt(sum(b^2))
